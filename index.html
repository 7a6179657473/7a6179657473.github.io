The first conversation

Most small businesses don't have a security policy. Not a bad one — none at all. They don't know which laptops have EDR installed, whether those endpoints are pulling updates, or what happens to their data when an employee walks out the door. The first step isn't buying software. It's understanding what you actually have.

Asset identification

You can't protect what you can't see. Asset identification isn't a technical exercise — it's a business one. Which employee is using which device? Is that device managed? Does it meet the policy you haven't written yet? These are the questions that determine your actual attack surface.

Disaster recovery planning

A breach isn't a matter of if. A DRP answers the question your team will ask at 2am when something goes wrong: what do we do right now? Who calls who? What systems get isolated, what gets preserved, and how do we get back online without making things worse?

Proportional security

Full enterprise security implementation is expensive. Good baseline security isn't. MFA, a written policy, a documented asset list, and a recovery plan — these are not glamorous. But they are the difference between a bad day and a business-ending event. That's the conversation worth having.

The goal

If I can walk out of a meeting having convinced a business owner that MFA alone will likely save everything they've built — that's a productive day.

H1 Hactivity Monitor

Bug Bounty Recon Pipeline

A two-tool Python recon suite built for continuous bug bounty operations. The H1 Hactivity Monitor polls the HackerOne API in real time, surfacing newly disclosed vulnerability reports as they go public. Extended into a full automated recon pipeline — subfinder enumerates subdomains, httpx probes live hosts, and ProjectDiscovery notify pushes categorized asset-change alerts to Discord every 4 hours. Diffs results against saved baselines to surface only what actually changed: new subdomains, services appearing or disappearing, scope shifts.

Engineered for reliability: flock-based locking prevents overlapping runs, automatic state archiving preserves baseline history, and the systemd unit applies filesystem sandboxing and privilege restrictions — minimizing attack surface on the host itself.
